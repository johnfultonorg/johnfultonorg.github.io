AARP Top Suggestions


For Avoiding Scams

AARP suggests watching for these common red flags:

  • Pressure to act immediately before you can think or verify details.
  • Insistence on gift cards, wire transfers, or cryptocurrency for payment.
  • Unsolicited contact via phone, email, or text requesting personal details.
  • Offers that sound too good to be true—prizes, sweepstakes, or investment gains requiring upfront payment.
  • Requests for personal information such as Social Security numbers, passwords, or account numbers.

For Protecting Yourself and Your Information

  • Shred sensitive documents: Use a cross-cut shredder for anything containing financial or personal information.
  • Monitor your credit: Check reports regularly and consider a free credit freeze.
  • Secure devices and accounts: Use strong, unique passwords and enable MFA.
  • Be wary of unexpected calls: Don’t answer unknown numbers; use “silence unknown callers.”
  • Avoid public Wi-Fi for sensitive tasks: Never access banking or personal accounts over unsecured networks.

For Assisting Loved Ones

  • Start a conversation: Discuss scams calmly; be aware of new “friends” or pressure from others.
  • Find a trusted contact: Encourage adding a trusted contact on financial accounts to be alerted to suspicious activity.
  • Help with reporting: Assist loved ones in reporting fraud to banks, police, the FTC, and the FBI’s IC3 portal.

For Victims of a Scam

  • Report immediately: Contact your bank, credit card provider, or website involved; file at IdentityTheft.gov.
  • Call the Fraud Watch Network Helpline: 877-908-3360 for free assistance.
  • Seek emotional support: AARP and Volunteers of America offer peer discussion groups for fraud victims.
