5 Tips to Identify a Phishing Email


1. Check the Sender’s Address Carefully

  • Phishing emails often pretend to be from trusted sources (banks, schools, government offices), but the sender’s email address is slightly wrong.
  • Watch for look-alike domains, such as support@paypa1.com instead of support@paypal.com.
  • If anything looks off, do not click links or reply; instead, go directly to the organization’s official website.

2. Watch for Urgent or Threatening Language

  • Phishers try to create panic or urgency so you act without thinking.
  • Common examples include messages like: “Your account will be locked in 24 hours!” or “Immediate action required!”
  • Legitimate organizations rarely force you to make instant decisions by email; when in doubt, verify using a trusted phone number or website.

3. Inspect Links Before Clicking

  • Hover your mouse over any link (without clicking) to preview the actual web address (URL).
  • If the link doesn’t match the company’s real domain, looks extremely long, contains random characters, or uses an unfamiliar domain, treat it as suspicious.
  • When unsure, type the organization’s official address directly into your browser instead of using the email link.

4. Look for Spelling, Grammar, and Formatting Errors

  • Professional organizations usually proofread their messages and use consistent branding.
  • Watch for poor grammar, awkward or machine-like phrasing, and obvious spelling mistakes.
  • Strange logos, odd colors, or layout problems can also be clues that the email is not legitimate.

5. Don’t Trust Unexpected Attachments or Requests for Sensitive Info

  • Phishing emails often include attachments that contain malware or links to fake login pages.
  • Be very cautious if an email asks for passwords, Social Security numbers, bank details, or other sensitive information.
  • Legitimate organizations will not ask you to send this type of information by email; contact them through a verified channel if you are unsure.
